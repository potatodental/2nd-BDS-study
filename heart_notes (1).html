1 Overview

📐 Anatomy
  • Location: Middle mediastinum
  • Shape: Hollow, conical muscular organ
  • Size: AP 6 cm · Transverse 8–9 cm · Length 12 cm
  • Weight: 230–340 g
  • Chambers: 2 atria + 2 ventricles
  • Apex: Left ventricle → 5th ICS, midclavicular line, directed downward, forward, left
  • Base: Posterior — right atrium (1/3) + left atrium (2/3), directed backward and right
⚙️ Physiology
  • Function: Acts as two pumps in series — right heart (pulmonary) + left heart (systemic)
  • Cardiac output: ~5–6 L/min at rest
  • Transport: O₂, nutrients, hormones, waste removal
  • Also regulates: Temperature, internal environment
  • Both ventricles must pump equal amounts over time — maintained by Frank–Starling mechanism
🔗 Anatomy → Physiology The heart's conical shape with apex pointing left and down means the apex beat is palpable at the 5th ICS midclavicular line — this is where you auscultate the mitral valve. The two-pump arrangement in series means pulmonary and systemic outputs must always match — a structural design that forces balanced circulation.

Layers of Pericardium

LayerAnatomyPhysiology / Function
Fibrous Pericardium Outer tough fibrous sac · Conical shape · Apex blends with great vessels · Base fused with central tendon of diaphragm Prevents overdistension of heart · Anchors heart in mediastinum · Limits sudden volume changes
Serous Pericardium — Parietal Inner lining of fibrous pericardium · Mesothelium Smooth surface reduces friction during heartbeat

Pericardial Space Between parietal and visceral layers Contains thin capillary fluid layer → lubricates · Reduces friction during cardiac cycle
🔗 Anatomy → Physiology
  • The fibrous pericardium limits overfilling — in acute heart failure, rapid fluid accumulation in the pericardial space (cardiac tamponade) compresses the heart because the stiff fibrous layer cannot expand
  • Inflammation of pericardium = pericarditis → painful friction rub heard on auscultation
  • Fluid aspiration routes: parasternal or subcostal — these avoid lung and coronary vessels

3 Chambers of the Heart

Comparison of Four Chambers

ChamberAnatomyPhysiology
Right Atrium Quadrilateral · Rough anterior part (pectinate muscles) + smooth posterior (sinus venosus) · Contains fossa ovalis (remnant of foramen ovale) · Receives SVC, IVC, coronary sinus Receives deoxygenated blood from systemic circulation · Passes blood to right ventricle via tricuspid valve · Contains SA node (in sulcus terminalis region) → pacemaker
Right Ventricle Triangular · Thinner wall (4–5 mm) · Contains trabeculae carneae, papillary muscles · Outflow = infundibulum (conus arteriosus) Pumps deoxygenated blood into pulmonary trunk · Low pressure pump (~25 mm Hg systolic) · Right ventricular failure → systemic venous congestion
Left Atrium Quadrangular · Thicker wall than right (~3 mm) · Receives 4 pulmonary veins · Forms base of heart (2/3) Receives oxygenated blood from lungs · Acts as reservoir during ventricular systole · Low-pressure filling chamber
Left Ventricle Conical shape · Wall 3× thicker than RV (8–12 mm) · Contains 2 papillary muscles · Outflow = aortic vestibule · Forms apex of heart Pumps oxygenated blood into aorta · High pressure pump (~120 mm Hg systolic) · Left ventricular failure → pulmonary congestion
🔗 Anatomy → Physiology
  • Wall thickness difference (LV 8–12 mm vs RV 4–5 mm) reflects their pressure work — LV pumps against 120 mm Hg systemic resistance vs RV against 25 mm Hg pulmonary resistance
  • Fossa ovalis in right atrium = remnant of foramen ovale; if patent (PFO) → right-to-left shunt and cyanosis
  • Trabeculae carneae increase surface area and prevent suction of endocardium, important for efficient ventricular ejection
  • Interatrial septum separates atria — defect (ASD) causes left-to-right shunt initially
  • Interventricular septum: membranous (posterosuperior, small) + muscular (rest) — VSD most common at membranous part

4 Valves

AV Valves (Atrioventricular)

📐 Anatomy
  • Tricuspid: Right AV valve · 3 cusps (anterior, posterior, septal) · Circumference ~10–12 cm
  • Mitral (Bicuspid): Left AV valve · 2 cusps (anterior, posterior) · Circumference ~7–9 cm
  • Cusps attached to papillary muscles via chordae tendineae
  • Papillary muscles: 3 in RV, 2 in LV
⚙️ Physiology
  • Open toward ventricles — allow atria → ventricle flow
  • Close at start of ventricular systole → prevent backflow
  • Closure produces S1 (LUB) heart sound
  • Papillary muscles contract during systole → prevent valve cusps from prolapsing into atria

Semilunar Valves

📐 Anatomy
  • Pulmonary valve: At junction of RV and pulmonary trunk · 3 cusps (2 anterior, 1 posterior)
  • Aortic valve: At junction of LV and aorta · 3 cusps · Thicker than pulmonary cusps
  • Aortic sinuses of Valsalva above each cusp → coronary arteries arise here
⚙️ Physiology
  • Open away from ventricles — allow blood out during systole
  • Close when ventricles relax → prevent backflow from arteries
  • Closure produces S2 (DUB) heart sound + dicrotic notch in aortic pressure

Auscultation Areas Anatomy + Heart Sounds Physiology

🔗 Anatomy → Physiology
  • The smaller circumference of mitral valve (7–9 cm vs tricuspid 10–12 cm) makes it more prone to stenosis — mitral stenosis is the most common valvular disease
  • Aortic sinuses of Valsalva (anatomical dilations above aortic cusps) → coronary ostia open here → during diastole when aortic valve closes, blood fills sinuses and enters coronaries — the anatomy ensures coronary perfusion occurs in diastole
  • Chordae tendineae prevent valve prolapse — if ruptured (e.g. in MI) → acute mitral regurgitation

5 Cardiac Muscle (Myocardium)

📐 Anatomy — Structure
  • Ribbon-like fibres (not cylindrical like skeletal muscle)
  • Branched and interdigitate with each other
  • Intercalated discs at junctions → tight junctions (structural) + gap junctions (electrical)
  • Richly supplied by capillaries (1 capillary/fibre)
  • Central nucleus, abundant sarcoplasm
  • Well-developed sarcotubular system — T-tubules at Z lines (one triad per sarcomere)
  • Striations present (like skeletal, unlike smooth)
⚙️ Physiology — Action Potential
  • RMP: −85 to −95 mV
  • Duration: ~250 ms (much longer than nerve/skeletal)
  • 5 phases with a characteristic plateau (Phase 2)
  • Phase 0: Rapid depolarization (Na⁺ influx + Ca²⁺)
  • Phase 1: Brief rapid repolarization (K⁺ out)
  • Phase 2: Plateau — slow Ca²⁺ in via L-type channels
  • Phase 3: Repolarization — Ca²⁺ channels close, K⁺ out
  • Phase 4: Resting potential maintained by Na⁺–K⁺ ATPase

Cardiac Muscle Properties

PropertyPhysiological MeaningSignificance
AutomaticityAbility to generate its own impulse without external stimulationHeart continues beating even when nerves are cut
RhythmicityGenerates impulses at regular intervalsMaintains regular heartbeat (~72/min)
ConductivityPropagates impulse through gap junctions — acts as functional syncytiumCo-ordinated contraction of entire atria then ventricles

Key Concept — Long Refractory Period: The absolute refractory period of cardiac muscle lasts 180–200 ms — almost as long as the entire contraction. This makes tetanic summation impossible. The heart MUST relax, fill, then contract again. Without this, the heart couldn't function as a pump.
🔬 Excitation–Contraction Coupling (How electrical signal becomes mechanical force)
Step 1 — ElectricalAction potential in sarcolemma → spreads into T-tubules

Step 2 — Trigger Ca²⁺T-tubule depolarization → opens L-type Ca²⁺ channels on T-tubule membrane → Ca²⁺ enters from ECF

Key difference from skeletal muscle Cardiac muscle depends on extracellular Ca²⁺ (from ECF via L-type channels) in addition to SR Ca²⁺. This is why cardiac contractility is sensitive to plasma Ca²⁺ and drugs like digoxin (which raises intracellular Ca²⁺ by blocking Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase).
🔗 Anatomy → Physiology
  • Intercalated discs with gap junctions → low-resistance bridges → action potential spreads rapidly through the entire atrial or ventricular syncytium → co-ordinated "all-or-none" contraction of whole heart
  • T-tubules at Z-lines in cardiac muscle (vs A-I junction in skeletal) → ensures Ca²⁺ release is synchronized with sarcomere shortening
  • Rich capillary supply (1 per fibre) → heart has very high O₂ demand (8 mL/100 g/min at rest) → must extract 70–80% of O₂ from blood even at rest → any decrease in coronary flow → immediate ischaemia

6 Conducting System

📐 Anatomy — SA Node Located in right atrium, just right of SVC opening · Contains P-cells (pacemaker cells) · Supplied by right vagus + right sympathetics
↓ impulse spreads through atrial muscle + internodal pathways (Bachmann, Wenckebach, Thorel) simultaneously
⚙️ Physiology — Pacemaker Potential RMP only −55 to −60 mV (not stable) · Slow Na⁺ leak → gradual depolarization = pacemaker potential (prepotential) · T-type Ca²⁺ channels open → reaches threshold −40 mV → action potential generated at 70–80/min
↓ 0.03 s to reach AV node
📐 Anatomy — AV Node Right side of posterior atrial septum, near tricuspid valve, above coronary sinus · Supplied by left vagus + left sympathetics
↓ AV nodal delay = 0.1 s (slow conduction)
⚙️ Physiology — AV Delay Delay allows atria to complete contraction and fill ventricles BEFORE ventricles contract · Without delay, ventricles would contract before filling is complete
↓
📐 Anatomy — Bundle of His From AV node → through fibrous skeleton → divides into right + left bundle branches at crest of muscular interventricular septum
↓
📐 Anatomy — Purkinje Fibres Terminal fibres from bundle branches · Spread to all parts of ventricular myocardium · Largest fibres in heart (fast conduction)
↓ 0.08–0.15 s — ventricular depolarization complete
⚙️ Physiology — Ventricular Depolarization Sequence Starts at left side of interventricular septum → across middle septum → down septum to apex → returns along ventricular walls to AV groove (endocardial to epicardial)

Intrinsic Firing Rates (Why SA node dominates)

Pacemaker SiteIntrinsic RateClinical Significance
SA Node70–80/min (fastest)Normal pacemaker — dominates all others
AV Node40–60/minTakes over if SA node fails → junctional rhythm
Atrial muscle40–60/minEctopic atrial pacemaker
Ventricular muscle20–40/minVentricular escape rhythm — last resort, slow and dangerous
🔗 Anatomy → Physiology
  • The fibrous skeleton of the heart (fibrous rings around valves) electrically insulates atria from ventricles — the only path for impulse transmission is through the AV node and Bundle of His. This is why the AV delay is so critical — without it, atria and ventricles would activate simultaneously
  • The anatomy of Purkinje fibres (largest diameter = fastest conduction) ensures ventricles depolarize from apex upward → blood ejected upward toward outflow tracts efficiently
  • When AV node is blocked → complete heart block — ventricles beat independently at 20–40/min (Stokes–Adams attack)

7 Electrocardiogram (ECG)

📐 Anatomy basis ECG records potential differences from body surface. Body fluids are good conductors (electrolytes). The heart acts as a dipole — depolarized part = negative pole, non-excited = positive pole. Waves recorded depend on orientation of lead relative to heart.

8 Cardiac Cycle

⚙️ Overview Duration: 0.8 s at HR 75/min · Systole: 0.3 s · Diastole: 0.5 s · At high HR, diastole shortens much more than systole → reduced coronary perfusion and filling

  • Papillary muscles contract at start of systole simultaneously with ventricular walls → prevents mitral/tricuspid prolapse into atria during the high-pressure systolic phase
  • The fibrous AV ring pulls down during systole (vis-a-fronte) → enlarges atrial lumen → sucks blood from great veins (ventricular systolic suction)
  • Ejection fraction = 65% normally (80 mL out of 130 mL EDV) — reduced in heart failure

9 Cardiac Output & Regulation

Cardiac Output Basics

⚙️ Formula
  • CO = SV × HR
  • Normal: 70–80 mL × 70 beats = ~5–6 L/min
  • Cardiac index: CO / body surface area = 3.2 L/min/m²
  • Ejection fraction: SV/EDV × 100 = ~65%
⚙️ Determinants
  • Preload (EDV) → stretches fibres → Frank–Starling
  • Afterload (peripheral resistance) → opposes ejection
  • Heart rate → directly proportional to CO (within limits)
  • Contractility → intrinsic myocardial force (sympathetics, Ca²⁺, drugs)

Frank–Starling Law

⚙️ Physiology Within physiological limits, the force of cardiac contraction is proportional to the end-diastolic volume (preload). More stretch → more force. This is called heterometric autoregulation.
🔗 Clinical Relevance
  • If LV fails and blood backs up → EDV increases → Frank–Starling boosts contraction → temporarily compensates. But excessive stretch → descending limb → decompensated heart failure
  • This mechanism also ensures equal output from both ventricles — if RV pumps slightly more, LV fills more and automatically pumps more
🔄 Extrinsic Regulation — Autonomic Control of Cardiac Output
DivisionEffect on HREffect on ContractilityMechanism
Sympathetic↑ (positive chronotropic)↑ (positive inotropic)β₁ receptors → ↑ cAMP → ↑ Ca²⁺ entry · Norepinephrine/epinephrine
Parasympathetic (Vagal)↓ (negative chronotropic)↓ atria only (no effect on ventricles)M₂ receptors → ↑ K⁺ conductance → hyperpolarization · Acetylcholine
Vagal tone dominates at rest — resting HR ~72/min. Atropine (blocks vagus) → HR rises to 150–180/min. When both are blocked → intrinsic rate ~100/min.

10 Coronary Circulation

Right Coronary Artery (RCA)

📐 Anatomy
  • Arises from right aortic sinus of ascending aorta
  • Between pulmonary trunk and right auricle
  • Branches: right conus, SA artery, right ventricular rami, posterior interventricular branch
  • Supplies: RV, RA, posterior LV, posterior IVS, SA node (60%), AV node

Left Coronary Artery (LCA)

📐 Anatomy
  • Arises from left posterior aortic sinus of ascending aorta
  • Wider and larger than RCA
  • Branches: LAD (anterior interventricular) + circumflex
  • Supplies: Anterior LV, LA, anterior IVS, left bundle branch
  • LAD = "widow maker" — supplies most of LV

Venous Drainage

📐 Anatomy
  • Coronary sinus (2–3 cm, in posterior AV groove) → drains most of heart → opens into right atrium between IVC and tricuspid valve
  • Tributaries: Great cardiac vein, middle cardiac vein, small cardiac vein, posterior vein of LV, oblique vein
  • Also: anterior cardiac veins + Thebesian veins (direct into chambers) = physiological shunt

Coronary Blood Flow — Physiology

⚙️ Characteristic Features
  • Normal flow at rest: ~250 mL/min (70 mL/100 g/min) = 5% of cardiac output
  • O₂ extraction: 70–80% at rest (vs 25% whole body) → cannot extract more → increased demand MUST be met by increased flow
  • During exercise: 3–6× increase in coronary flow
  • LV flow occurs mainly in diastole — systolic ventricular contraction compresses intramural vessels → flow nearly zero during isovolumic contraction
  • RV flow occurs in both systole and diastole — lower ventricular pressure, less compression
🔗 Anatomy → Physiology
  • Coronary arteries are functionally end arteries (though anatomically anastomosing) → blockage causes infarction of that territory
  • Aortic sinuses of Valsalva ensure coronary ostia receive blood during diastole (when aortic valve closes and sinuses fill) — this is the anatomical design for coronary perfusion
  • At high heart rates → diastole shortens disproportionately → coronary perfusion time decreases → risk of subendocardial ischaemia in diseased hearts
  • Autoregulation maintains coronary flow constant from 70–200 mm Hg arterial pressure — fails below 70 mm Hg

11 Nerve Supply of the Heart

Sympathetic Supply

📐 Anatomy
  • Preganglionic: T1–T5 segments of spinal cord
  • Postganglionic: From superior, middle, inferior cervical ganglia + T1–T5 thoracic ganglia
  • Right side → mainly SA node
  • Left side → mainly AV node
  • Supplies: Atria, ventricles AND conducting system
⚙️ Effect
  • ↑ Heart rate (positive chronotropic)
  • ↑ Conduction velocity (positive dromotropic)
  • ↑ Excitability (positive bathmotropic)
  • ↑ Force of contraction (positive inotropic)
  • Vasodilation of coronary arteries
  • ↑ Cardiac output

Parasympathetic Supply (Vagus)

📐 Anatomy
  • Preganglionic: Nucleus ambiguus + dorsal nucleus of vagus (medulla)
  • Right vagus → mainly SA node
  • Left vagus → mainly AV node
  • Supplies: Atria and conducting system ONLY (not ventricles)
  • Postganglionic in cardiac plexus
⚙️ Effect
  • ↓ Heart rate (negative chronotropic)
  • ↓ Conduction velocity (negative dromotropic)
  • ↓ Force of contraction of atria only
  • ↓ Coronary blood flow
  • No effect on ventricular contractility
🔗 Anatomy → Physiology
  • Vagal fibres supply only atria and conducting system — not ventricles. This means vagal stimulation can slow/stop SA node and block AV conduction (complete heart block) but cannot stop ventricular contraction once impulse escapes below AV node
  • Referred pain of MI: Cardiac afferent pain fibres travel with sympathetic nerves (T1–T5) → pain referred to left arm, jaw, neck — these dermatomes correspond to T1–T5 — purely anatomical explanation for clinical symptom
  • Normal resting HR 72/min is lower than intrinsic SA node rate (~90–100/min) → vagal tone slows it down. Cut vagus → HR rises. Block vagus with atropine → HR rises to 150–180/min
